Project

Penny Hockey

Tabletop Game Board

CAD Fabrication Arduino

Overview

A compact penny hockey game board built to focus on clean mechanics, smooth play, and a durable surface.

Design

The Penny Hockey Project was a very interesting and fun project, incorporating the many skills learned throughout the year. My project features the NASA logo in the center of the board laser engraved in clear plexiglass. This board is surrounded by a wooden frame, supported by dado cuts and assembled using miter joints nailed together. All these were cut on the table and miter saws. The goals for the penny to slide through were made by first drilling large holes in each corner and then using the jigsaw, and finally inserting 3d printed parts to allow the penny to slide smoothly. The 3d printed parts are also found in the feet and the holder for all the electronic components. I chose to 3d print these parts due to the ease of manufacturing and the ability to design the part to the exact form necessary, allowing the electronics to fit snugly in a small form factor. Finally, the LEDs and electronic components were soldered and wired together using solid core wire, which I chose due to its ability to retain its shape. Overall, this project was fun to create and challenging as well, significantly improving many skills I have learned prior. For instance, the 3d printed components have improved my 3d modeling capabilities due to the need for exact dimensions and form. Furthermore, these parts have also taught me many ways of assembling components, such as snap fitting, gluing, nailing, and screwing. Another aspect in which this project has improved my skills is using the Arduino. Due to the need to manage multiple components, such as the LEDs, LCDs, piezo, and pushbuttons simultaneously, I needed to learn an important concept: multitasking with the Arduino. Since the Arduino can only process one instruction at a time, I have learned how to write code to get around this and manage multiple components seemingly simultaneously. Finally, this project has also improved my soldering and wiring skills. I enjoyed the Penny Hockey Project a lot, and the skills learned through this will help me build more complex projects in the future.

Problem Statement:

A pressing problem that has developed recently is that more and more children are being drawn to smartphones and tablets. This is likely to continue as products are evolving to have more addictive and engaging features. Because of this trend, many children are suffering from attention deficiency and many other issues that can be directly attributed to looking at a digital screen for extended periods of time, as well as problems such as decreased social interaction and decreased physical activity that are indirectly caused by this.

Design Statement:

Design a fun and engaging board game that incentivizes them to play the game instead of spending time on digital devices. This is done through the use of colors, lights, and engaging gameplay.

Constraints

  • must be completed individually

  • additional, non-standard materials must be documented with annotated concept sketches
  • walls must be ¾” wide by 1 ¼” tall
  • must be within the $30 budget

Research

I first conducted research on prior designs and rules of penny hockey sets made by other people. I decided to use a wooden frame and include “players” to block shots from certain angles, as well as rounded inside corners. However, while most penny hockey sets include a flat wooden base and a hole for the penny to fall through, I took inspiration from air hockey tables and decided to use a plexiglass surface with legs and a slit for the penny to slide through in the walls. I also decided to use a LCD display with buttons to keep the score and LED lights for more visual interest, as well as a buzzer for sound.

I then had to learn and research the tools I would use to create this project. This includes the safety and operational guidelines, which I found from the Glenbrook South Engineering website. (GBS Engineering, n.d.).

Research

Two concept drawings—one orthographic and one isometric—of my project. This includes dimensions.

CAD model

I designed and modelled my project using Autodesk Fusion 360. All parts and assemblies were modelled and dimensioned accurately by me, except for the electronic components—the LCD (j.jacck44, 2020); Arduino (big_red_frog, 2014); and breadboard (3dmakernow, 2019)—were imported.
