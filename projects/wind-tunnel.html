Project

Wind Tunnel

Subsonic Aerodynamic Research

March-May 2025 Aerodynamics CAD

Abstract

Many aspiring STEM students who may be interested in aerodynamics do not have a way to learn and study it, especially aerodynamic forces and airflow around objects. Therefore, our goal was to design and develop a tool that can be used to understand aerodynamics in an intuitive and engaging way. It should be constructed to be affordable and accessible, and its operation should not be extremely complicated.

Design

Our wind tunnel uses foam board and acrylic windows for the main tunnel section with a 3D printed adapter to connect the fan. Straws were used to straighten the airflow, which ensures the incoming air flows straight through the tunnel with minimal turbulence. For the test stand, we used two load cells to measure force and a servo motor to control the angle of the test object. These were connected to an Arduino, allowing us to control and visualize the forces on the object. Also, to visualize the airflow, a smoke generator was used. However, a challenge was that the airflow was too fast even on the lowest fan setting, resulting in unavoidable turbulence in the airstream, which caused the smoke to disperse and be faint. Another challenge was attaching all the straws, which took a significant amount of time. Overall, I learned many things throughout this project. I learned how to use load cells to measure forces and then plot the data using an Arduino, creating a real time graph of the forces. Also, I learned how to use the laser cutter to cut materials. Finally, my 3D modelling and 3D printing skills have significantly improved as a result of this project since it uses a variety of 3D printed parts. If I were to do anything differently, I would use a fine honeycomb mesh instead of individual straws, and I would use a larger smoke generator with a higher smoke output.

Build & Fabrication

The tunnel was constructed using 3/4" plywood for the outer shell and acrylic panels for the test section viewing windows. Honeycomb flow straighteners and screens were installed upstream of the contraction to reduce turbulence.

  • Laser-cut plywood components for precise geometry
  • Custom-designed 3D-printed corners and mounting brackets
  • Pressure-tapped test section for aerodynamic measurements
  • Variable-speed motor controller for flow control

Results & Testing

Initial calibration tests showed excellent flow uniformity with velocity variation under 3% across the test section. The tunnel has been successfully used to test airfoil models and measure lift/drag coefficients.

Challenges & Lessons

  • Flow separation: Required multiple iterations of diffuser angle to prevent separation
  • Vibration: Added rubber isolation mounts to reduce motor vibration
  • Sealing: Used weatherstripping and silicone to eliminate air leaks
