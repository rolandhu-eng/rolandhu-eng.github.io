# 01. Overview

Most fascinating airflow phenomena are invisible to the naked eye. Schlieren imaging makes density gradients in air visible by turning tiny refraction differences into contrast. The same effect causes shimmering distortions near hot pavement; this setup makes that behavior strong enough to see even warm air from body heat.

---

# 02. Principle

A point light source reflects off a concave mirror and ideally focuses back into a point image. If air in front of the mirror has different densities, the changing index of refraction bends some light away from that focal point. Placing a filter or small cutoff near the focus turns that missing light into visible dark regions in the image.

---

# 03. Setup

The setup used a 114 mm diameter spherical telescope mirror with a 900 mm focal length. For the light source and camera, an iPhone flashlight and camera can be used. Longer focal lengths generally improve sensitivity.

Alignment was the main challenge, so a 3D-printed kinematic mount was designed. A central pivot allows angular motion, while three screws provide fine adjustment. The tradeoff is that linear adjustments still require moving the full setup, and horizontal adjustment can require turning two screws together.
